Weekly Computer Science Update

>> STATUS: ANALYSIS_COMPLETE

Last week provided valuable insight into how students are progressing across our Computer Science courses. In several areas, students demonstrated strong technical ability. In others, we identified opportunities for growth that go beyond coding syntax and focus more on problem interpretation, attention to detail, and effective use of class time.

This update is intended to keep you informed about where students are leveling up, where challenges are emerging, and what instructional adjustments are being made to best support their learning.

>> System_Log: Course_Performance

AP Computer Science A

Students recently completed a full-length practice AP exam, offering a helpful snapshot of current readiness. On the multiple-choice portion, scores ranged roughly from 50% to 95%, with two students achieving nearly perfect results. This confirms that many students have developed strong foundational knowledge.

The Free Response (FRQ) portion, however, revealed a consistent challenge: students often struggled to fully follow instructions and meet all stated requirements. Importantly, this does not indicate a lack of coding ability, but rather a gap in problem-solving and interpretation skills.

As we move into Unit 3, instruction will emphasize slowing down, analyzing problems thoroughly, and asking clarifying questions.

  • Reading and interpreting problems carefully
  • Matching solutions precisely to requirements
  • Independent practice outside of class (via new coding platform)

Honors Computer Science 1

CS1 students are making excellent progress with functions in Python. Syntax understanding is strong across the board. The primary challenge now is translating word problems into complete, well-structured solutions—specifically identifying variables, returning values correctly, and formatting output.

In many cases, student logic is sound, but small formatting details (spacing, punctuation) prevent solutions from passing automated grading. These details matter in real-world technical work. The Unit 9 Study Guide will be released later this week.

  • Careful attention to output formatting
  • Breaking down word problems step by step
  • Beginning the Unit 9 Study Guide

Advanced Cloud Computing (AWS)

Students began the AWS AI/ML Foundations course. Overall, they are following the material well. However, to ensure grades meaningfully represent learning—rather than the ability to retry knowledge checks indefinitely—I am adjusting the grading policy.

I will no longer use built-in AWS knowledge checks as standalone grades. Instead, module grades will be based on:

  • Completion of required AWS content
  • Accuracy and effort on supplemental assignments designed/assessed by me

This approach ensures support is provided where it is genuinely needed as we explore task automation and intelligent assistants.

>> SYSTEM ALERT: Classroom Focus & Integrity

One challenge that became more pronounced last week was student distraction during class time. When students choose to use Computer Science time to study for other courses or complete unrelated work, it creates two significant issues:

  1. They miss instruction and do not complete work designed to be done during class.
  2. Unfinished work becomes homework, often resulting in zeros that cannot be made up once a unit closes.

This has been most noticeable in Advanced Cloud Computing. Moving forward, classroom expectations will be enforced more consistently. District-approved consequences will be applied when necessary—not as punishment, but as preparation for professional environments where disengagement has real consequences.

Note: Students will not be permitted to take district-funded certification exams unless they can demonstrate genuine understanding of the material.
